## 140 Decibels of Human Emotion

There is an instrument in China that can reach 140 decibels — louder than a rock concert, louder than a jet engine at 100 meters, loud enough to cause permanent hearing damage if played indoors without protection. It is played at weddings and funerals, at temple festivals and political rallies, at moments of supreme joy and deepest grief.

This is the **唢呐 (suǒnà)** — the Chinese double-reed horn that has announced births, marriages, victories, and deaths for over 1,500 years. If you've heard any Chinese film set before the modern era, you've heard the suona. It is the sound of China itself — raw, piercing, and impossible to ignore.

## Born on the Battlefield

The suona originated in Persia (modern-day Iran) as a military signaling instrument, arriving in China via the Silk Road around the 3rd century CE. Its original name, *surnay*, survives in Persian and Arabic. The Chinese adopted it, Sinicized the name, and made it their own.

What distinguished the suona from earlier Chinese wind instruments was its power. Flutes (dizi) were gentle. Mouth organs (sheng) were mellow. The suona could be heard across a battlefield. It could rally troops from a kilometer away. It could strike terror into enemies who had never heard anything like it.

By the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644), the suona had transitioned from military to civilian use. Every village had a suona player — often hereditary, passed from father to son. He was summoned for every significant life event: a birth (high, bright tones), a wedding (fast, celebratory ornamentation), a funeral (low, mournful, sustained notes that seemed to stretch toward infinity).

## The Technique That Breaks Reeds

The suona uses a double reed — two thin pieces of cane that vibrate against each other when the player blows through them. This is the same mechanism as the oboe or bassoon, but the suona's reed is smaller, harder, and placed on a metal tube that flares into a conical brass bell.

Playing the suona requires extraordinary lung capacity. Professional players practice circular breathing — inhaling through the nose while continuously exhaling through the mouth — to sustain notes that can last over a minute. The embouchure (mouth position) must be tight enough to produce the characteristic piercing tone but flexible enough to bend notes, add vibrato, and execute the rapid ornamental figures that define suona style.

The reeds wear out quickly. A professional might go through 3–5 per performance. Each reed costs $2–$5 and lasts about 2 hours of playing time. Suona players carry reed-making kits — scissors, cane strips, binding wire — and can fashion a working reed from raw materials in under five minutes. This self-sufficiency developed out of necessity: in rural China, music stores didn't exist, and if your reed broke mid-ceremony, you fixed it yourself or you failed the community.

## Two Pieces Define Everything

The suona repertoire contains hundreds of pieces, but two define the instrument's emotional range:

**百鸟朝凤 (Bǎiniǎo Cháofèng)** — "A Hundred Birds Paying Homage to the Phoenix": A virtuoso showpiece where the suona imitates the calls of dozens of bird species — magpies, cuckoos, orioles, sparrows — building to a climax where the "phoenix" (represented by the highest, brightest register) sings above them all. The piece requires techniques including glissando (sliding between notes), flutter-tongue (rolling the tongue to produce a trill effect), and double-tongue (alternating tongue positions for rapid articulation). It is the suona equivalent of a piano concerto — technical display wrapped in naturalistic imagery.

**江河水 (Jiānghé Shuǐ)** — "River Water": A funeral piece of devastating emotional weight. Played slowly, in the lowest register, with long sustained notes that bend downward like someone crying. There are no bird calls here, no virtuosity for its own sake — only grief expressed through breath and reed. When played well, audiences weep. When played at actual funerals, even the strongest men have been known to collapse.

## The Instrument That Refuses to Die

Like many Chinese folk instruments, the suona faced extinction during the Cultural Revolution. It was condemned as backward, superstitious, and associated with feudal customs. Suona players were persecuted. Instruments were confiscated and destroyed.

But the suona survived underground. Village musicians hid their horns. Secret performances continued at night. And when restrictions lifted in the late 1970s, the suona returned with a vengeance — now amplified through electric speakers, now fused with rock bands, now featured in film scores by composers like Tan Dun.

In 2013, the documentary *Suan Tian* ("Making Heaven Weep") introduced the suona to global audiences, following a family of suona masters across generations. The film won awards at international festivals and proved that this "primitive" instrument had artistic depth that transcended cultural boundaries.

Today, young Chinese musicians are rediscovering the suona not as a relic but as a voice — one that can cut through modern noise, that demands attention, that refuses to be background music. In a world of carefully curated, politely mixed audio experiences, the suona is a reminder: sometimes music should be too loud. Sometimes it should hurt a little. Sometimes that's exactly what you need to feel alive.
