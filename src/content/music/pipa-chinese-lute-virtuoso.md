## Five Fingers, Two Thousand Years

The pipa has four strings and no picks — you play it with all five fingers of your right hand simultaneously. This is not a limitation. It is an arsenal. The signature technique called *lunzhi* (轮指) rotates all five fingers in rapid succession, producing a tremolo so fast it makes flamenco guitar look leisurely. Top pipa players can sustain over 20 notes per second.

The instrument arrived in China during the Han Dynasty via the Silk Road — the name "pipa" comes from the Persian *barbat*. Over two millennia, Chinese musicians transformed it from a foreign curiosity into the most technically demanding plucked instrument in the world. The standard repertoire includes "Ambush from Ten Sides" (十面埋伏), a 20-minute programmatic work depicting an entire battle — advancing armies, ambushes, the clash of weapons, and the final defeat — all rendered in sound by a single player on four strings.

## The Woman Who Brought It to the World

Wu Man is the pipa's greatest ambassador. A graduate of the Central Conservatory of Music in Beijing, she moved to the United States in 1990 and spent three decades performing with orchestras, collaborating with composers like Philip Glass and Terry Riley, and proving that the pipa belongs on the world stage. Her rendition of "White Snow in Spring" (阳春白雪) at the Metropolitan Museum of Art is a masterclass in how ancient music can feel startlingly modern.

The pipa's range is astonishing. It can whisper like a zither and roar like a war drum. It can play courtly Tang Dynasty court music and avant-garde contemporary compositions. In the right hands, four strings contain multitudes.
