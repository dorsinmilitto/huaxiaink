## 1930s Shanghai, Remixed for the 21st Century

In the 1930s, Shanghai was the Paris of Asia — a city where Chinese folk melodies collided with American jazz, Russian orchestras, and French chanson in the dance halls of the French Concession. The result was "Shidaiqu" (时代曲) — "songs of the era" — a genre that was already a fusion before anyone invented the word.

The Shanghai Restoration Project, created by producer Dave Liang, takes those 1930s recordings and reimagines them with electronic beats, hip-hop production, and live Chinese traditional instruments. Erhu lines weave over house rhythms. Pipa tremolos punctuate trip-hop grooves. A singer from the 1930s, her voice crackling with vinyl warmth, duets with a modern jazz vocalist.

## China Has Always Remixed

There is a tendency in the West to treat "traditional Chinese music" as something pure, ancient, and unchanging — a museum artifact that modern artists either preserve or corrupt. This is wrong. Chinese music has been a remix culture for two thousand years.

The pipa came from Persia. The erhu came from Central Asia. The suona came from Arabia. Every instrument now considered "traditionally Chinese" arrived via the Silk Road and was adapted, modified, and Chinese-ified over centuries. The Tang Dynasty absorbed music from across the known world. The 1930s Shanghai jazz era was just the latest iteration of a tradition that has always been about absorbing the new and making it Chinese.

## The Future Sound of the Past

Electronic music and Chinese traditional instruments are not opposites. They are complementary technologies. Electronic production offers infinite timbral palette and rhythmic precision. Traditional instruments offer human expression, microtonal nuance, and two thousand years of accumulated musical wisdom. The best fusion projects — Shanghai Restoration Project, GONG, Ziyue — do not dilute either tradition. They show that the space between them is larger and more fertile than anyone assumed.
