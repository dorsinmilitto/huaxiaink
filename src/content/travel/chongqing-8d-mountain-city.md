## 32 Million People You've Never Heard Of

There is a city in China with more people than Tokyo, more skyscrapers than New York, and a topology that makes GPS systems simply give up. Its name is Chongqing, and chances are you've never heard of it.

That's by design, not accident. Chongqing spent most of its history as a forgotten river port — too mountainous for farming, too remote for trade, too foggy for anyone to care. Then, in 1997, China's central government did something extraordinary: they drew a boundary around an area the size of Austria, gave it direct municipal status, and said: *build*.

What happened next defied every assumption about urban planning.

## The City That Grew Up Instead of Out

Most cities spread horizontally. Chongqing had no choice but to grow *vertically*. Built on a peninsula where the Yangtze and Jialing rivers converge, surrounded by mountains that rise 500 meters on every side, flat land simply doesn't exist. So the city went up — and kept going up, and then built another layer on top of the first one, and then connected them with bridges that look like they were designed by M.C. Escher.

The result is what the internet calls an "8D city" — a term born from viral videos showing roads that loop over themselves, buildings with entrances on the 10th floor, subway trains that pass *through* residential apartment blocks, and pedestrian bridges that connect rooftops six stories above the street below.

It's not a gimmick. It's what happens when 32 million people need to live somewhere that physics said they shouldn't.

## The Monorail That Goes Through a Building

Chongqing's Line 2 monorail is perhaps the single most photographed transit moment in China. The train passes directly through the 6th-to-8th floors of a residential building — the Liziba Station — with apartments above and below the tracks. Residents report the noise is surprisingly minimal. Engineers call it "a creative solution to a spatial problem." The rest of the world calls it insane.

But this is how Chongqing thinks. When you can't go around, you go through. When you can't build next to, you build on top of. When the mountain says no, you build the mountain into your architecture.

## Cyberpunk After Dark

At night, Chongqing transforms. The fog catches the neon. The skyscrapers glow in tiers — red, blue, white, gold — stacked against the dark mountainside like a circuit board designed by a civilization that skipped the Industrial Revolution and went straight to the future.

The Hongyadong stilt-house complex, 11 stories of traditional wooden architecture rebuilt in modern materials, cascades down the cliff face lit like a lantern. Below it, the Jialing River reflects a thousand lights. Above it, elevated highways thread between towers.

It is, by any aesthetic measure, the most cyberpunk city on Earth — not because it was designed to be, but because extreme density, extreme topography, and extreme speed of development *produced* cyberpunk as a byproduct. Chongqing didn't set out to look like Blade Runner. It just happened to have all the ingredients.

## The Traffic That Broke Navigation

There is a joke in China: "In Chongqing, never trust your GPS." The city has over 10,000 bridges and flyovers. Roads have been known to fold back on themselves three times within 200 meters. A single intersection can have five levels. Taxi drivers navigate by landmark and intuition — because algorithms, trained on flat cities, have no category for "road that goes over a building that goes over a river that goes over another road."

In 2023, a viral dashcam video showed a driver taking 12 minutes to navigate what appeared to be a single intersection. It accumulated 4.7 million views. For Chongqing residents, this wasn't viral content — it was Tuesday.

## Why Chongqing Matters

Chongqing is the answer to a question the world hasn't fully asked yet: what happens when you build a megacity in the most impossible geography, with the most impossible speed, for the most impossible number of people?

The answer is: it works. The subway runs. The food is legendary. The nightlife is electric. The 32 million people go about their lives with the same mundane rhythm as any city dweller anywhere — except their commute might involve a train through a building, a bridge over a river that's also over a road that's also over a mall.

Chongqing proves that human adaptation has no ceiling. Given enough necessity and enough will, we will build a city anywhere — even on a mountain, even in the fog, even when the laws of physics politely suggest we don't.
