## The River That Painted Itself

There is a place in southern China where the ground decided to grow upward instead of staying flat. Limestone peaks — thousands of them — rise from flat farmland like teeth from a green gum, each one covered in subtropical vegetation, each one a different shape: some like cones, some like camels, some like old men leaning on canes, some like nothing you have ever seen before.

Between these peaks flows the **漓江 (Lì Jiāng)** — the Li River. It is 83 kilometers of water that has inspired more paintings, poems, and photographs than almost any other landscape on Earth. The 20-yuan banknote features it. UNESCO designated it. And every year, millions of travelers float down its length on bamboo rafts, trying to understand how reality can look this much like imagination.

## Karst Geography on an Impossible Scale

The Li River flows through one of the world's largest karst landscapes — terrain formed by the dissolution of soluble bedrock (limestone) over approximately 300 million years. Rainwater, slightly acidic from atmospheric carbon dioxide, seeped into cracks in the limestone and dissolved it away, leaving behind the harder rock as peaks, caves, underground rivers, and natural bridges.

What makes the Li River karst unique is its density. In the area between Guilin and Yangshuo — the most famous stretch of the river — there are estimated to be over 10,000 individual karst peaks within a 2,000-square-kilometer area. They range from 30 meters to 500 meters tall. Some are so steep that they cannot be climbed. Others have temples built into their cliff faces. Many contain caves with stalactites that have been growing for millions of years.

The river itself is shallow — averages 1–3 meters deep — but wide enough for tourist boats up to 40 meters long. The water is famously clear in dry seasons and emerald-green after rain. Its color changes hourly with the light: silver at dawn, turquoise at noon, gold at sunset, indigo under moonlight.

## The Journey: Guilin to Yangshuo

The classic Li River cruise covers 83 kilometers between Guilin (the regional capital) and Yangshuo (the smaller county town that has become a backpacker mecca). The journey takes 3–4 hours by motorized boat or 90 minutes by high-speed ferry. But the authentic experience is the **bamboo raft** — actually PVC pipes lashed together with plastic chairs bolted on top — poled by a local boatman who knows every current, every hidden rock, every spot where the reflection of the peaks creates a perfect circle in the water.

Key sights along the way:

**Elephant Trunk Hill**: A limestone formation near Guilin that resembles an elephant drinking from the river. It is the city's official symbol and appears on virtually every Guilin postcard ever printed.

**Nine Horse Fresco**: A cliff face whose natural discoloration patterns are said to resemble nine horses. Legend says that ordinary people see three horses, clever people see five, and only the wisest can see all nine. Most tourists claim to see seven.

**Xingping**: A village along the river that appears unchanged since the Ming Dynasty. Its stone arch bridge, terraced rice fields, and cormorant fishermen (who use trained birds to catch fish) appear in countless photographs. This is the exact view featured on the 20-yuan note.

**Yangshuo**: The endpoint, where the river widens and the peaks become more scattered. The town itself is a study in contrasts: ancient streets lined with craft shops alongside neon-lit bars serving Western breakfasts to hungover backpackers.

## The Artists Who Couldn't Stop Looking

Chinese landscape painting (*shanshui hua* — literally "mountain-water painting") developed during the Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE) and reached its peak during the Song Dynasty (960–1279). The Li River region was always a favored subject. Painters came here not to copy what they saw but to internalize it — to learn the spirit of mountains and water so they could recreate it from memory in studios hundreds of kilometers away.

The most famous painter associated with the region is Fan Kuan (c. 990–1030), whose masterpiece *Travelers Among Mountains and Streams* depicts towering peaks rising from mist — a composition directly inspired by the karst geography of Guangxi. The painting hangs in the National Palace Museum in Taipei and is considered one of the greatest achievements of Chinese art.

But the Li River's influence extends beyond traditional art. Impressionist painters who encountered Chinese landscape prints in the late 19th century adopted similar compositional techniques. Modern photographers treat the Li River as the ultimate testing ground for exposure control — the contrast between bright sky, dark rock, reflective water, and white mist challenges even the best cameras.

## What Changes, What Stays

The Li River has been commercialized. Tourist boats discharge waste into the water (though regulations have improved dramatically since 2015). Bamboo rafts now use noisy diesel engines instead of silent poling. The cormorant fishermen who once worked the river for a living now pose for photos for $5 per shot.

And yet: stand on any riverbank at dawn, before the boats arrive, when the mist still clings to the peaks and the only sound is water against stone, and you will understand why people have been coming here for 1,500 years. Not because it's beautiful — though it is — but because it feels like a place where the boundary between earth and sky, between solid and fluid, between real and imagined, dissolves completely.

That is what the Li River offers: not just a view, but a way of seeing.
