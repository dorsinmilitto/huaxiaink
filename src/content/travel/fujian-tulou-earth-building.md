## A Village That Is Also One House

Imagine a circular building six stories tall, with walls three meters thick at the base tapering to half a meter at the top. Inside, concentric rings of rooms radiate outward from a central courtyard where an entire community gathers — cooking, washing clothes, drying grain, celebrating weddings, mourning deaths, resolving disputes. Eight hundred people live here. They share one entrance. One ancestral hall. One well in the courtyard that has provided water for four hundred years.

This is a **土楼 (tǔlóu)** — literally "earth building" — and it is one of the most remarkable architectural forms ever created. Found exclusively in the mountainous border regions of Fujian Province in southeastern China, tulou were built by the **Hakka** people — "guest families" who migrated southward across China over centuries of war and persecution, carrying their architecture with them like portable homeland.

## How You Build a House That Lasts 700 Years

Tulou construction is a feat of engineering that uses nothing but local materials: rammed earth, wood, stone, and tile. Here is how it works:

**Foundation**: Builders dig a circular or rectangular trench 3 meters deep, fill it with stones and lime mortar, and let it set for months. The foundation must be deeper than any earthquake has ever cracked the ground in this region.

**Walls**: Rammed earth is made by pouring a mixture of soil, sand, and lime (sometimes mixed with sticky rice juice for binding) between wooden forms, then pounding it with heavy wooden rammers until it becomes denser than concrete. Walls are built in layers, each layer allowed to partially dry before the next is added. A wall 1.5 meters thick takes months to build but will last centuries without maintenance.

**Structure**: Wooden pillars support the interior floors and roof. The outer earthen wall bears no structural load — it is purely defensive. Between the wall and the inner wooden structure is a corridor running around each floor, providing both circulation and an additional buffer against attack.

**Roof**: Tiled roofs slope inward toward the central courtyard, collecting rainwater into the well below. This design serves two purposes: water conservation in a region prone to drought, and fire protection (wet tiles don't burn).

A single large tulou took 3–5 years to build with a team of 50–100 workers. The cost was shared among all families who would live inside. Every household contributed labor proportional to the number of rooms they would occupy.

## Defense by Design

Every feature of the tulou reflects the Hakka people's history as perpetual outsiders in hostile territory:

**One entrance**: Most tulou have exactly one door, reinforced with iron plates and stone frames. Above the door, openings allow defenders to pour boiling water or drop rocks on attackers.

**No windows on lower floors**: Ground and second-floor exterior walls are solid earth — impenetrable to arrows, bullets, or climbing. Windows appear only on the third floor and above, too high for easy access and small enough to shoot out through but not enter through.

**Central well**: The courtyard well provides water during sieges. Some tulou have secret tunnels connecting to external water sources.

**Food storage**: Lower floors are used for grain, livestock, and weapons storage. Upper floors are living quarters. During attacks, residents could survive for months on stored provisions.

These defenses were tested repeatedly. Bandit raids were common in the Ming and Qing Dynasties. Warlord conflicts in the early 20th century threatened many communities. Japanese forces occupied parts of Fujian during WWII but never successfully breached a major tulou compound. The buildings did exactly what they were designed to do: protect the vulnerable inside.

## UNESCO Recognition and the Tourism Paradox

In 2008, UNESCO inscribed 46 Fujian tulou sites on the World Heritage List, calling them "exceptional examples of a building tradition and function exemplifying a particular type of communal living and defensive organization." The designation brought international attention — and tourism.

Today, the major tulou clusters (Hongkeng, Hekeng, Chuxi) receive millions of visitors annually. Entrance fees fund preservation. Local families earn income from guesthouses, restaurants, and selling handicrafts. Young people who had left for cities are returning because tourism makes rural life economically viable again.

But there is tension. Traditional tulou were designed for privacy — the inward-facing layout means every family's activities are visible to everyone else. Tourists walk through courtyards where people are still living, cooking, raising children. Some residents have moved out, renting their rooms to hotels. Others stay and adapt, finding ways to monetize hospitality while preserving dignity.

The question facing tulou communities is the same one facing heritage sites worldwide: how do you share something precious without destroying the conditions that made it precious?

## What the Teaches About Living Together

In an era when most humans live in households of 2–4 people, the tulou represents a radically different model: 80–800 people sharing walls, water, space, and fate. Conflicts are inevitable. So are resolutions — because you cannot move away from your neighbor when your home is also theirs.

The Hakka people didn't choose this arrangement because they loved communal living. They chose it because survival required it. And in requiring it, they discovered something that modern urban planners are only beginning to relearn: that density, when designed with intention, creates community in ways that sprawl never can.

Visit a tulou today, and you'll find WiFi, air conditioning, and satellite dishes mounted on 600-year-old earthen walls. The contradiction is the point. These buildings were never meant to be museums. They were meant to be lived in. And as long as people live in them, they remain alive — not relics of the past, but ongoing experiments in how humans might dwell together.
