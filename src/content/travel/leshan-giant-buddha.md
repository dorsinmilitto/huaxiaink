At the point where three rivers converge — the Min, the Qingyi, and the Dadu — a giant sits. He has been sitting for **1,300 years**. His head touches the sky at **71 meters** tall. His shoulders span **28 meters**. His ears are **7 meters** long. His toenails are large enough to seat a person.

This is the **Leshan Giant Buddha** (乐山大佛) — the largest stone Buddha statue in the world, carved directly into a cliff face at the confluence of three rivers in Sichuan Province.

## A Monk's Vow That Took 90 Years to Fulfill

In the year **713 CE**, a Buddhist monk named **Hai Tong** arrived at the junction of the three rivers. The waters here were treacherous — currents collided, whirlpools formed, and boats capsized regularly. Locals believed the rivers were haunted by water spirits.

Hai Tong had a different idea. He proposed carving a giant Maitreya Buddha into the cliff — not just as an act of devotion, but as an **engineering solution**. The rubble from the carving would be dumped into the river, altering the current and calming the waters.

The monk traveled far and wide to raise funds. When a corrupt official demanded the donation money, Hai Tong reportedly **cut out his own eye** and offered it, saying: "I would rather give you my eye than the Buddha's money." The official fled.

Hai Tong died before the project was completed. His disciples continued the work. Two generations later, in **803 CE**, the Leshan Giant Buddha was finished — **90 years** after it began.

## Hidden Engineering: The Buddha That Drains Itself

The Leshan Giant Buddha is not just a statue — it is a **hydraulic engineering masterpiece**. The Tang Dynasty craftsmen built a hidden drainage system into the sculpture itself:

- **Gutters** carved along the hair curls channel rainwater down the back
- **Hidden pipes** in the arms and legs divert water away from the face and body
- A **drainage layer** behind the statue prevents water accumulation

This system has kept the Buddha relatively well-preserved for over a millennium, despite being exposed to rain, wind, and river humidity. Without it, the soft sandstone would have eroded centuries ago.

## The Buddha That Changed a River

Hai Tong's engineering theory was correct. The massive amount of stone rubble dumped into the river during construction did alter the current. The treacherous confluence became navigable. Boat accidents dropped dramatically.

The Buddha did not just calm spirits — it calmed the river. In a very real, physical sense, the statue **saved lives** by redesigning the hydrology of the river junction. This makes Leshan unique among the world's great religious monuments: it was both a spiritual and a civil engineering project.

## What You See Today

Modern visitors can experience the Buddha from two perspectives:

- **From the river** (by boat): The full scale becomes apparent. The Buddha appears to emerge from the mountain itself — head at the treetops, feet in the water. Boats pause at his toes, and for a moment, every passenger falls silent.
- **From the cliff stairs**: A narrow staircase descends alongside the Buddha from head to foot, passing his enormous ears, his folded hands, and finally arriving at his toes. The descent takes about 30 minutes and is not for the faint of heart — the cliff drops away steeply on one side.

In 1996, UNESCO inscribed the Leshan Giant Buddha as a **World Heritage Site** as part of the "Mount Emei Scenic Area." In 2025, the site saw a record number of international visitors, driven partly by viral social media content showing the Buddha from drone perspectives.

## The Mountain Is a Buddha, and the Buddha Is a Mountain

There is a local saying: "The mountain is a Buddha, and the Buddha is a mountain" (山是一尊佛，佛是一座山). This is not just poetry. When viewed from across the river, the entire Lingyun Mountain range forms the shape of a reclining Buddha — with the Giant Buddha sitting at the heart position.

Whether this was intentional or discovered later is debated. But standing on a boat in the confluence of three rivers, looking up at 71 meters of stone that has watched over the water for 1,300 years, the question of intentionality seems to miss the point. The Buddha is there. The river is calm. The mountain is watching.
