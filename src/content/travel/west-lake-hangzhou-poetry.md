## The Lake That Made a Language

In 1089, the poet Su Dongpo was appointed governor of Hangzhou. The lake was choked with silt. He ordered 200,000 workers to dredge it, used the dredged mud to build a causeway, and planted willows along it. Then he wrote a poem comparing the lake to Xizi, the legendary beauty: "West Lake is like Xizi — always beautiful, whether in light makeup or heavy rain."

That single comparison defined West Lake for the next thousand years. Every subsequent poet, painter, and emperor who visited had to measure their experience against Su Dongpo's metaphor. The lake did not just inspire art. It became a standard against which beauty itself was judged.

## Ten Scenes, One Thousand Years

The "Ten Scenes of West Lake" is a curated list that dates back to the Southern Song Dynasty. Each scene is a moment — not a place, but a combination of place, time, weather, and light. "Lingering Snow on the Broken Bridge" only counts if you see the bridge after a snowfall, when the snow on the arch melts faster than on the railings, making the bridge look broken from a distance. "Three Pools Mirroring the Moon" requires the Mid-Autumn moon, when three pagoda-like stone towers in the water each hold candles that create multiple moon reflections.

This is landscape designed for poetry. The Song Dynasty emperor who commissioned the list understood that West Lake's power was not in its geology but in its frames — the specific ways of seeing that transformed water and hills into art.

## Dragon Well and the Taste of Place

West Lake is also the home of Longjing (Dragon Well) tea, China's most celebrated green tea. The tea bushes grow on the hillsides surrounding the lake, bathed in the same mist that gives the water its ethereal quality. The best Longjing is harvested before the Qingming Festival in early April — the "pre-Qingming" grade — when the leaves are so tender they can be pressed flat in a hot wok without breaking.

Drinking Longjing beside West Lake is drinking the landscape itself. The chestnut notes in the tea come from the soil. The sweetness comes from the lake mist. This is terroir as the French understand it, but documented a thousand years earlier.
