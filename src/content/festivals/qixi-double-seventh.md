## The Oldest Love Story Still Told

One star is Vega — the Weaver Girl (Zhinu). Another is Altair — the Cowherd (Niulang). Between them stretches the Milky Way, a river of light that keeps them apart for 364 days a year. On the seventh night of the seventh lunar month, a bridge of magpies forms across the sky, and the lovers meet for a single night.

This story is at least 2,600 years old. It appears in the Shijing (Book of Songs), compiled around 600 BC. By the Han Dynasty, it was already the subject of poetry, and during the Tang Dynasty it became the central narrative of the Qixi Festival — China's original Valentine's Day, celebrated centuries before Saint Valentine was born.

## Why Weaving Matters

The Weaver Girl is not just a romantic figure — she is the goddess of weaving, spinning, and needlework. Historically, Qixi was the day when women displayed their weaving skills, competed in threading needles by moonlight, and prayed for dexterity. The connection between love and craftsmanship is not accidental. In agricultural China, a woman's ability to weave was her economic independence. The festival honored both romance and competence.

## A Bridge of Living Birds

The magpie bridge is the story's most unforgettable image. In Chinese folklore, magpies are birds of joy and connection. The idea that thousands of them would fly to the heavens and form a living bridge for two lovers transforms the story from tragedy to hope. The separation is real, but so is the reunion.

Modern Qixi has been commercialized — restaurants offer couple's dinners, jewelry stores run promotions. But look up on the seventh night of the seventh month, and the two stars are still there, still separated, still meeting. The sky remembers what the shopping malls have forgotten.
