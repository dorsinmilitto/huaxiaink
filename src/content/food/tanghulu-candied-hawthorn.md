## The Sound of Winter

If you have walked through Beijing in winter, you have heard tanghulu before you have seen it. The sound is a hard crack — candy shell breaking against candy shell, a dozen skewers clattering together as the vendor carries them through the hutongs on a straw-poled stand.

Tanghulu is hawthorn berries on a bamboo skewer, dipped in molten rock sugar. The sugar cools into a glassy shell that shatters on the first bite, releasing an explosion of tart fruit underneath. The contrast is the point: hard candy versus soft fruit, sweet versus sour, winter cold versus warm sugar syrup.

## A Concubine's Cure

According to legend, tanghulu was invented for a Song Dynasty concubine who had lost her appetite. The emperor's doctors prescribed hawthorn — a traditional digestive aid — but she refused to eat it plain. A court chef dipped the berries in sugar, and the concubine recovered. Whether the story is true or not, the medical logic is sound: hawthorn aids digestion, and the sugar makes the medicine go down.

## The Sugar Glass Technique

Making tanghulu at home requires precision. You must heat rock sugar and water to the "hard crack" stage — about 300 degrees Fahrenheit. Too cool, and the coating is sticky. Too hot, and it burns. The test is simple: dip a spoon in the syrup and drop it into cold water. If it forms hard, brittle threads that snap cleanly, it is ready. Then you have about 30 seconds to dip each skewer before the sugar sets.

This is why tanghulu is a winter food. In summer humidity, the sugar shell absorbs moisture and becomes sticky within minutes. In the dry cold of a Beijing January, the candy stays crisp for hours. The season is not optional. It is structural.
