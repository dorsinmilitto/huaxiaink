## Soup Inside a Dumpling — How?

The first time you eat a xiaolongbao, you are unprepared. You bite into what looks like an ordinary dumpling, and a geyser of hot broth explodes into your mouth. The broth is pork-based, seasoned with ginger and Shaoxing wine, and it is hot enough to burn if you are not careful. This is not a design flaw. This is the point.

The secret is aspic — meat jelly mixed into the filling. At room temperature, the aspic is solid. When the dumpling is steamed, the aspic melts into soup, which is trapped inside the sealed wrapper. The wrapper must be thin enough to be translucent but strong enough to hold boiling liquid. This is why xiaolongbao chefs train for years.

## The Shanghai Standard

Xiaolongbao is associated with Shanghai, but its origins are disputed. Some trace it to Nanxiang, a town on Shanghai's outskirts, in the 1870s. Others claim an earlier pedigree. What is certain is that Shanghai made it famous. The classic version uses pork filling and pork aspic, but crab-and-pork is the luxury variant, available during autumn when hairy crabs are in season.

The proper way to eat xiaolongbao is a three-step ritual. First, lift the dumpling with chopsticks by its "nipple" — the twisted top where the wrapper is gathered. Second, place it on your spoon and nip a small hole in the side to release steam and sip the broth. Third, eat the dumpling with a dip of black vinegar and julienned ginger. Rushing step two is how you burn your tongue.

## Why It Matters

Xiaolongbao is not just food. It is an engineering problem solved with intuition and tradition. No machine can make them properly — the wrappers are too thin, the folding too delicate, the timing too precise. Every xiaolongbao you eat was made by hand, one at a time, by someone who spent years learning. In an age of automation, that is a radical act.
