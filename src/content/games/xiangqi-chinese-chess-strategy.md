## A Battlefield Between Two Palaces

Xiangqi — Chinese Chess — is not chess with different pieces. It is something older, wilder, and more direct. There is no queen. There are cannons that leap over other pieces to strike. There is a river that divides the board in two. And there is a general who may never leave a three-by-three palace, because a leader who runs onto the battlefield has already lost.

The board is 9 lines by 10, with a "river" between the fifth and sixth rows. Soldiers become more powerful when they cross it — they can move sideways as well as forward. The horse (not the knight) can be blocked by an adjacent piece, a rule that makes every horse move a calculation of timing and positioning. And the cannon — a piece with no equivalent in Western chess — captures by jumping over exactly one intervening piece, like a siege weapon firing over a wall.

## Why the Cannon Changes Everything

The cannon is what makes Xiangqi feel fundamentally different from chess. In chess, every piece captures by moving to the target's square. The cannon captures by *leaping* — it needs a "screen" piece in between. This single rule transforms strategy. A cannon behind a wall of friendly pieces is a threat that cannot be ignored. Two cannons coordinating can create crossfire that no defense can withstand.

Chinese military theory emphasizes indirect attack and positional advantage — Sun Tzu's "win without fighting." Xiangqi embodies this. The cannon is not a piece that fights head-on. It is a piece that exploits the geometry of the board, striking where the opponent is unprepared.

## A Game That Built a Civilization

Xiangqi is played by over 200 million people, mostly in China, Vietnam, and Chinese diaspora communities worldwide. Street games in Guangzhou and Chengdu can draw crowds of spectators who comment on every move. The Chinese Xiangqi Association runs professional leagues with sponsored teams and full-time players.

The game's history is entangled with Chinese military history. Some scholars trace it to the Warring States period (475-221 BC), when generals literally fought the battles that Xiangqi abstracts. Others point to the Han Dynasty, when the chariot — the most powerful piece on the Xiangqi board — was the most powerful weapon on the real battlefield. The game is not merely a metaphor for war. It is war, compressed into a grid.
