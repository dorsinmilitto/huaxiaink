## A Battlefield on Your Kitchen Table

There is a river running through the middle of every Xiangqi board. It has no water, no current, no fish — just two empty spaces marked "楚河" on one side and "汉界" on the other. But that river carries the weight of two thousand years of history.

The Chu-Han Contention (206–202 BC) was the war that birthed the Han Dynasty, one of China's greatest empires. Two warlords — Xiang Yu of Chu and Liu Bang of Han — fought a four-year struggle for supremacy. Xiangqi immortalized that conflict on a board, turning one of China's most defining historical moments into a game that anyone can play on a park bench.

## An Army at Your Fingertips

Xiangqi pieces are not carved figures. They are wooden or plastic discs stamped with Chinese characters, and each character tells you exactly what the piece does. The General (帅/将) commands from within a 3×3 palace. Advisors (仕/士) guard diagonally. Elephants (相/象) patrol the home side but cannot cross the river. Horses (馬) move in an L-shape — but can be blocked. Chariots (車) roar across the board in straight lines. Cannons (砲/炮) leap over other pieces to strike. Soldiers (兵/卒) march forward and, once across the river, gain the freedom to move sideways.

Notice something? There is no queen. Xiangqi predates the European queen's rise to power on the chessboard by centuries. Instead, the game rewards coordinated attacks — a cannon needs a screen, a horse needs an open path, and the general must never face the enemy general directly across an empty board. The rule is called "flying general," and it forces both players to maintain a buffer zone, echoing the缓冲地带 that real generals depend on.

## Street-Level Strategy

Walk through any Chinese park on a morning, and you will find Xiangqi games in progress. Men hunched over boards, arguing over moves, kibitzers offering unsolicited advice, the clack of pieces punctuating heated debate. Xiangqi is a working-class intellectual's game — no silk robes or scholarly pretension required.

The game's popularity endures because it mirrors something deeply Chinese: the belief that strategy is not a luxury but a survival skill. From Sun Tzu to the Three Kingdoms, Chinese history is built on the idea that brains beat brawn. Xiangqi distills that idea into thirty-two pieces on a board. Every move is a calculation. Every sacrifice has a reason. Every game tells a story about patience, timing, and knowing when to strike.

## A Living Tradition

Xiangqi remains one of the most played board games on Earth, with over half a billion players worldwide. Online platforms host professional leagues. China's Xiangxiang supercomputers analyze positions with the same intensity that DeepMind brought to Go. But unlike Go, which carries a spiritual weight, Xiangqi wears its heart on its sleeve — it is war, pure and simple, and everyone understands war.

The river is still there. The generals still face each other. And on a thousand park benches across China, the battle of Chu and Han is fought again every single day.
